Independent Research

Prototyping & Flight-Testing Biomimetic Wings to Optimize Aerodynamic Efficiency

Project Snapshot

With air travel emitting one billion tons of CO2 annually and transportation contributing ~28% of global emissions, developing efficient wing designs to reduce drag is critical for mitigating global warming. This two-phase study evaluated biomimetic wings inspired by eagles and humpback whales. In Phase 1, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) and wind-tunnel testing on 10-inch half-wing models revealed that the whale-mimetic design improved the lift-to-drag ratio by up to 47% at a 5-degree angle of attack and 50 m/s airspeed; the eagle-inspired design showed inconsistent performance. Phase 2 scaled the whale-mimetic design to a 40.5-inch full-wing model. CFD simulations at 10 m/s and a 5-degree angle of attack showed a 12.2% improvement in the lift-to-drag ratio. Subsequent flight testing of a drone prototype demonstrated a 12% increase in flight distance per volt compared to conventional wings. These results suggest that whale-mimetic wings significantly enhance fuel efficiency and represent a promising advancement in reducing the environmental impact of aviation.

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)

15,000 Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulation trials executed at airspeeds of 50 m/s, 100 m/s, and 240 m/s at angles of attack of 0, 5, and 10 degrees.

Custom-built Wind-tunnel Testing

A wind-tunnel was built and utilized for testing the best-performant whale-mimetic vs conventional aerofoil. Lift produced by the whale-mimetic aerofoil was compared against the lift produced by conventional cambered aerofoils.

Flight Testing & Telemetry Data

A commercial drone-sized prototype aircraft with a 40.5-inch wingspan was flown using the 3D printed conventional and whale-mimetic wings. This involved:

The Problem Aviation generates 1B tons of carbon annually. With demand projecting a 110% rise by 2050, innovative fuel efficiency solutions are critical to combat global warming.
The Hypothesis Implementing humpback whale flipper tubercles or slotted eagle primary feathers into aerofoil designs minimizes drag, thereby increasing lift-to-drag (L/D) ratios and fuel efficiency.
Research Background
  • Adapting aerodynamic features from nature, such as bird and whale anatomy, to enhance airplane wing design
  • Humpback whale flipper tubercles reduce drag and improve the lift-to-drag ratio
  • Eagle wingtip feathers minimize drag by breaking large vortices into smaller, less impactful ones
Objectives
  • 1
    Identify the optimal biomimetic wing design for improved aerodynamic efficiency
  • 2
    Prototyping and Flight-Testing Biomimetic Wings: Empirical Performance Evaluation
Methodology - Phase 1

3D Modeling & Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD): Designed 10-inch half-wing models representing conventional Clark-Y, whale-mimetic, and eagle-mimetic configurations. Conducted over 200 CFD simulations across 15,000 trials, analyzing performance at varying airspeeds (50, 100, 240 m/s) and Angles of Attack (0°, 5°, 10°).

Wind Tunnel Construction & Empirical Testing: To validate computational models with physical data, a custom 2'x1.5'x1.5' wind tunnel was fabricated. The setup utilizes 280 six-inch PVC pipes arranged in a honeycomb formation to ensure laminar airflow at a controlled speed of 4 m/s.

Methodology - Phase 2

UAV Fabrication & 3D Prototyping: Transitioned from computational testing to physical validation by scaling the most efficient models to a 40.5-inch full wingspan. Fabricated custom aircraft platforms incorporating the whale-mimetic tubercles directly into the leading edge of the aerofoils for real-world environmental testing.

Flight Testing & Telemetry Tracking: Conducted rigorous field tests at operational speeds of ~10 m/s. Programmed and integrated a custom onboard data logger to precisely capture flight telemetry and power consumption. The physical flight data corroborated the CFD simulations, demonstrating a +12% increase in overall flight range (1802 meters/volt versus the 1596 meters/volt baseline).

Result: 10-Inch Half Wing Simulation

Aerodynamic Efficiency Gains: The computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis revealed substantial performance improvements for the biomimetic designs. At a baseline testing speed of 50 m/s and an Angle of Attack (AoA) of 5°, the whale-mimetic tubercle wing demonstrated a peak +47% increase in the Lift-to-Drag (L/D) ratio compared to the conventional Clark-Y aerofoil.

Delayed Flow Separation: Visual airflow trajectory models confirmed the mechanism behind this efficiency. The leading-edge tubercles acted as passive flow control devices, channeling the air into accelerated streams (vortices) over the wing surface. This energized the boundary layer, successfully mitigating turbulence and significantly delaying flow separation and aerodynamic stall at higher angles of attack.

Result: 40-Inch Full Wing Simulation

Full-Scale Aerodynamic Validation: Following the successful half-wing trials, the fully scaled 40.5-inch UAV wingspan was subjected to comprehensive CFD analysis. The simulation data confirmed that the biomimetic tubercles successfully scale to larger geometries, maintaining significant aerodynamic superiority over the baseline models under real-world operational flight conditions.

Statistical Performance Consistency: To ensure reliability, variance in the aerodynamic data was rigorously tracked across multiple trials. The resulting statistical boxplot illustrates a tightly clustered performance metric for the biomimetic design. This indicates not just an improvement in the peak lift-to-drag ratio, but a highly stable and consistent aerodynamic profile essential for reliable UAV field flights.

Result: Flight Testing

Custom Telemetry Integration: To capture precise empirical data during physical flight tests, a custom data acquisition circuit was engineered and integrated directly into the UAV payload. This system tracked real-time voltage, current, and GPS distance simultaneously, ensuring that performance metrics collected during the ~10 m/s field tests were accurate, bias-free, and perfectly synchronized.

Empirical Range Extension: The physical field tests successfully validated the computational projections. The UAV equipped with the whale-mimetic leading-edge tubercles achieved a verified flight efficiency of 1802 meters per volt, compared to the 1596 meters per volt baseline of the conventional wing. This translates to a definitive +12% real-world flight range extension, underscoring the practical viability of biomimetic structures for commercial aviation.

Findings
  • The whale-mimetic wing had higher lift-to-drag ratio compared to the control and eagle-mimetic wing
  • Lift-to-drag ratio gain was most significant at lower airspeeds
  • Lift-to-drag gain decreased with larger wing size
  • Fuel efficiency gain observed in flight testing is proportional to the CFD lift-to-drag ratio gain

Limitations & Mitigations

Limitations

  • Wind tunnel airspeeds were limited to ~4 m/s, preventing the replication of high-speed flight conditions.
  • Physical drag could not be measured in the wind tunnel due to instrumentation constraints; only lift was recorded.
  • Manual pilot control and slight environmental winds introduced natural variability during flight testing.
  • Statistical analysis could not be conducted on flight test data due to a limited sample size of 20 flights exceeding 100 meters.

Mitigations

  • Environmental and pilot variables were significantly mitigated by flying models back-to-back on the same days using the same battery pack.
  • Flights less than 100 meters were removed to cleanse the data.
Conclusion
  • Whale-mimetic wings significantly improve lift-to-drag ratios and overall fuel efficiency over conventional designs.
  • Integrating these biomimetic structures into commercial UAVs tangibly extends their operational flight range.
  • Increased range capabilities accelerate commercial UAV adoption, replacing high-emission delivery vehicles and reducing net greenhouse gas output.
  • This approach represents a highly actionable step toward mitigating the broader environmental impact of the aviation and transportation sectors.

Destination Imagination

Combining Engineering, Art, and Teamwork on a Global Stage

The Ascent of the Thought-Tree

A huge sigh of relief washed over me as our colorful "thought-tree" finally rose into the air. As it reached the peak of its ascent, the circular leaves—embedded with custom LED strips—automatically lit up the room in a rainbow of colors. Attached to each leaf was a tag representing a core value of humanity: creativity, honesty, bravery, and kindness. While most teams at the competition built standard jigsaw puzzles, my team decided to build a "growing puzzle tree". We wanted the different branches to represent life abstracts, turning our project into a "tree of humanity".

Engineering Behind the Art

This was all part of Destination Imagination (DI), a STEAM competition where teams are given a massive challenge and have to use a mix of STEM, fine arts, and teamwork to build a solution and present it through a live skit. That year, our task was to build a puzzle solver robot that could solve a chosen puzzle using technical methods. I co-led the design and development of the technical side, working alongside friends who were just as obsessed with engineering and science as I was.

Bringing our story to life meant dividing up the work based on what we were good at, from writing the script to building the actual props. One of my friends was amazing at electrical engineering and circuitry, while another was a woodworking expert. Even though I was a key player in the overall technical build, I had my own learning curves. I seriously struggled with precisely cutting wood blocks and attaching them together with L-brackets. My teammate was patient enough to teach me, and in return, I taught them how to use the Arduino IDE to program our crane's controls. To make the mechanism work, we built an electromagnetic crane using programmed motors and switches, alongside a custom electromagnet to place the puzzle pieces. Then, to actually make the tree "grow," we built a pneumatic pump and piston system using hydraulics to lift the entire heavy structure up by six inches.

Precision Bean bag launcher

For another year's competition, the requirement was to build a beanbag launcher that could hit an exact distance with repeatable precision. We pooled our engineering skills and worked as a tight unit to design and calibrate a custom thrower mechanism. Our main goal was consistency, which meant analyzing launch angles and spring tension until the device worked like clockwork. After hours of fine-tuning the math and hardware, we got the launcher to a point where it could reliably and accurately fire a single beanbag right into a target zone between 280 and 300 centimeters away.

A Global Triumph

Watching our masterpiece actually work and amaze the audience and judges made me smile, knowing that months of research, trial-and-error, and pure determination were going to pay off. The teamwork skills I built during that DI season have been invaluable ever since, whether I'm working on a regular group project at school or collaborating with PhD students at the UT Austin aerospace lab.

Our team went on to be incredibly successful, ultimately winning the first-place trophy at the global competition twice—once in the middle school division and again in high school. Along the way, we were honored with both the DaVinci Award and the Renaissance Award, special recognitions reserved for teams that display exceptionally unique solutions, courageous risk-taking, and outstanding creativity.

Standing on top of a global stage that featured over 600 teams from 13 different countries was completely exhilarating. It wasn't just because we successfully combined art and engineering, but because we got to fail, learn, and succeed together as close friends.

My Aerospace Journey

From childhood wonder to real-world engineering.

The Spark

That night, the moon wasn’t the bright white I knew it to be; it was blood-red, and I had to know why. As a kindergartner, I couldn’t grasp how something in the sky could change so drastically overnight. When I learned about the mechanics of a lunar eclipse the next day, it felt like a door had unlocked.

Realizing there was an entire universe past our planet waiting to be understood changed everything for me. Ever since, I’ve been completely hooked on the mysteries of space.

When I got my first telescope at the age of six, I spent countless evenings in the backyard observing the craters of the Moon, searching for Mars, and charting the stars. Naturally, this deep-seated obsession began to spill over into every part of my childhood—from astronaut Halloween costumes and towering Lego rockets to a Mars-themed birthday cake.

Problem Solving & Space Camps

As I grew older, that passive wonder evolved into a desire to actually solve problems. This shift started at a NASA summer camp where I found myself designing simulated lunar bases and programming rovers. I loved it so much that I later returned as an assistant counselor to help spark that same excitement in younger kids.

Years later, I found myself standing as the flight director in a Space Shuttle simulator at the U.S. Space & Rocket Center. Staring at my monitor during a high-stakes scenario, I blurted out, “Whoa! Why are the payload bay doors closed?” Ordering CAPCOM to open them was a split-second decision that averted a simulated collision. Guiding my ten-person mission control team through those frantic moments left me with a clear, concrete ambition: one day, I want to help guide humanity’s real steps onto Mars.

Engineering the Future

I’m drawn to aerospace engineering because it does two things at once: it pushes the absolute limits of human endurance, and it creates a trickle-down effect of everyday innovations on Earth. But I also know that getting there requires solving massive hurdles first—like the immense weight and cost of fuel needed to break gravity compared to the relatively weak thrust of current chemical engines. Later, inspired by how effortlessly marine life moves through water, I taught myself 3D modeling and Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to explore biomimicry in flight. I designed aircraft wings modeled after the unique tubercles of a humpback whale and built a prototype remote-controlled plane to test the geometry in the real world. Seeing a 12% improvement in flight range was an incredible feeling—proof that looking to nature could dramatically cut drag and improve fuel efficiency.

Lately, I've been eager to push my boundaries even further. I currently assist a professor at the University of Texas at Austin's aerospace department on a project for the U.S. Department of the Navy, which has been an incredible opportunity to learn advanced aerodynamics directly from experts. Balancing my time in the lab with my high school aerospace coursework and NASA’s High School Aerospace Scholars program has been a whirlwind, but it’s continually grounding my technical foundation in real-world engineering.

Aerospace engineering has this amazing capacity to redefine what is humanly possible. I look forward to entering college to figure out my exact specialization, collaborate with peers who are just as obsessed with flight as I am, and dive into original research. Driven by the very same curiosity I felt as a kid looking up at a red moon, I'm ready to dedicate my college years to solving the complexities of spaceflight and helping make life beyond our planet a reality.
